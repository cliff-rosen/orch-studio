# Knowledge Artifacts — theme notes

*Working folder for pieces that share a single thesis.*

## The shared claim

Important knowledge needs to live in **durable, inspectable artifacts** — not in the generative medium that produced it.

The generative medium is powerful for discovery. It is the wrong substrate for the output to live in. The work that matters is the move *from medium to artifact*: from conversational transcript to maintained canon, from implicit LLM judgment to named operational structure.

## Pieces in this folder

### From Conversation to Canon
- **Medium:** chat conversations (plus project-style grouping).
- **Problem:** exploratory thought stays trapped in transcript form; no hardening step.
- **Artifact it argues for:** a maintained canonical representation — concepts, claims, distinctions, assumptions, tensions, open questions — separate from the transcript corpus and versioned over time.
- **Human vs. machine labor:** machines do extract / cluster / normalize / compare; humans do editorial adjudication (same? refinement? promote to canon?).

### Encoding Human Expertise
- **Medium:** implicit operational judgment — the LLM's, or the expert's own.
- **Problem:** expertise is treated as one thing ("we'll encode it") when it's many flavors, each with a distinct artifact shape and a distinct failure mode when missed.
- **Artifacts it argues for:** task graphs, decision trees, precondition lists, rubrics, recognizer-response tables, state schemas, constraint lists, handoff protocols. One artifact shape per flavor of expertise.
- **Human vs. machine labor:** experts name the flavor and produce the artifact; the orchestration is built around the artifacts, not around the LLM's judgment.

## Why they belong together

Both pieces:
1. Start by naming a substrate that is *good at generating* but *bad at storing* what matters.
2. Argue that the load-bearing knowledge must be moved out of that substrate into explicit, named artifacts.
3. Use **inspectability** and **auditability** as the payoff criterion. You should be able to point at the artifact and ask: is this right? is it current? where did it come from? how has it changed?
4. Position the human role as editorial / judgment-bearing and the machine role as mechanical / generative.

The canon piece is about this move on the **knowledge side** (what the thinking is *about*).
The expertise piece is about this move on the **operational side** (how the work gets *done*).

Together they cover the two places where implicit structure silently costs you: in what you know, and in how you work.
