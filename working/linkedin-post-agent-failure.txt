There are exactly five properties that determine whether an AI agent will succeed or fail — before the model even runs.

Every agent call depends on instructions, context, and tools. Those inputs need to be cogent. And cogency breaks down into five testable properties, in order:

Consistent — do the inputs align with each other? Do they hold up against what's plausible in the real world?

Correct — are they right for the actual goal?

Complete — can the agent execute the full task without hitting a gap?

Sufficient — is there enough for the right answer in this specific case?

Dense — is the signal clean enough for the model to find what matters?

The first three are hard but catchable. Contradictions show up. Wrong targets become obvious. Gaps reveal themselves in execution.

Sufficiency is the one that gets you.

Your inputs are consistent, correct, and complete. The agent runs the entire task, produces polished output, and misses the thing that actually matters — because the specification didn't know to ask for it. A contract analysis that covers every criterion you listed and misses the indemnification clause that's the actual landmine. Nothing signaled a gap. The output looks great. It's useless for the decision.

The model had no way to know. The inputs looked whole. You only catch this if someone who understands the domain reviews the result and asks the right question.

This is why agent reliability is dominated by system design, not model capability. And why the people designing the inputs need to understand the problem at a level the model never will.
