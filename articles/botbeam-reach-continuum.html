The Reach Continuum: Why AI's Next Unlock Is Physical

By Cliff Rosen, Founder & CTO, Orchestrator Studios

March 2026

LLMs in isolation are good at answering questions. That was the story in 2020. But an LLM without integration into the world around it is a very partial story. Remove all integration friction — give an agent full two-way reach into every domain it needs to touch — and the picture is transformatively different from what we had five years ago.

Getting to agent-intermediated life requires two things. First, reach — bridging the gap between the LLM and every domain it needs to interact with, in both directions. Second, reliability and security — making agents trustworthy enough that you'd actually let them run parts of your life. This article is about the first. (We address the second in our work on agent failure diagnostics and cogent agent design.)

Since ChatGPT launched in November 2022, AI agents have progressively expanded their reach through a series of stages. At every stage, there's a gap between what users want to do with AI and what's technically feasible. Forces on both sides close that gap. On the demand side, user appetite and awareness grow — every new capability raises expectations for the next one. On the supply side, models improve, orchestration patterns mature, and interoperability standards emerge. Each unlock happens when those two curves converge enough that someone can build the thin connecting layer between them. The layer was always buildable in theory. The gap just hadn't narrowed enough for it to work in practice.

The Pattern So Far

Each stage follows the same structure: a domain the agent can't touch, technology that already exists in some form, demand forming on one side, infrastructure maturing on the other, and a thin connecting layer that's missing. Someone builds it. The gap closes. Within months, the new capability feels unremarkable. Then the dynamic starts again.

Closed (November 2022). The agent could reason, but it couldn't see. Its entire world was its training data — a frozen snapshot with a hard cutoff date. Impressive, but answers from the past.

The World (2023). Internet access. Browsing tools, search integrations, real-time retrieval. Web APIs had existed for decades. The thin layer was giving the agent plumbing and permission to use them. Within a year, an AI that couldn't access the internet felt crippled. Before that, nobody thought to complain.

Your Info — Read (2024). Personal data access. Google Drive, email, calendars, databases. MCP and similar protocols created the thin layer — a standardized way for agents to discover and use existing APIs. The agent went from knowing about the world in general to knowing about your world in particular.

Your Info — Write (2024-25). Not just reading but acting. Send that email, create that event, update the spreadsheet. Same thin layer, write permissions. The technical barrier was minimal. The psychological barrier — trusting the agent to do things, not just see things — was significant. That trust built gradually, use case by use case.

Soft Physical (2025-26). Smart home edges. Play something on the speaker. Set a timer. Turn off the lights. Most people don't think of this as a frontier — it just works. But it's the first time the agent's reach extended beyond the purely digital.

There's a hidden constant across all five stages: every one of them sat on top of an IP ecosystem with 30 years of enterprise-driven interoperability behind it. HTTP, REST, OAuth, documented APIs for every major platform. The thin layer was thin because the foundation was thick. Three decades of integration work had already been done. The agent just needed a bridge to an already well-connected world.

Why Physical Is Actually Different

The physical world breaks this pattern in ways that matter.

Thirty years of fragmentation, not convergence. While the digital world spent three decades building interoperability over IP — with enterprise forces driving standards, open protocols, and documented APIs — the device world spent those same decades doing the opposite. On the commercial side: Crestron, Control4, proprietary lock-in as a business model. On the consumer side: HomeKit, Nest, Zigbee, Z-Wave, Alexa — every player building walled gardens. Nobody won. Nobody standardized. The integration mess isn't a temporary gap that's closing. It's the defining characteristic of the space.

Complexity kept it niche. Even where device control existed, adoption hit a ceiling — not a hardware ceiling, a UX ceiling. Wall panels gave way to smartphone apps, which was a step down in complexity, but still too many settings, too many apps, too much configuration. Smart home remained an enthusiast category. The technology worked. The experience of configuring and controlling it never became simple enough to go mainstream.

Generative AI breaks both barriers simultaneously. Natural language collapses configuration complexity to near zero. You don't learn the Hue app. You don't build automations in a rule engine. You just say what you want. This does something no previous technology did for the device world: it makes mass-market demand plausible. The same way "an AI that can't search the web" went from normal to crippled in twelve months, "an AI that can't interact with my home" will cross that same threshold. And once that expectation forms, it doesn't go back.

Generative AI is both the supply-side unlock — natural language as the universal device interface — and the demand-side accelerant — raising expectations for what your agent should be able to do. Both friction curves are converging. The window is opening.

The Window

This is a timing argument, not a technology argument. By roughly 2027, the large consumer electronics players will ship native AI agent integrations. Samsung is telegraphing this with SmartThings. Google has the hardware ecosystem and the AI capability. Apple has HomeKit. Amazon has Alexa. When these players arrive in force, the connecting layer becomes commodity infrastructure — but it will be commodity infrastructure within their walled gardens, deepening the fragmentation rather than resolving it.

The window is between now and then — perhaps 18 to 24 months. The opportunity isn't to build the definitive physical-AI platform. It's to build the thin layer first, learn what actually works, and establish position before the market commoditizes.

At every previous stage, the first mover wasn't the biggest player. Google didn't invent web browsing for AI — independent developers built browsing tools. Anthropic didn't build Google Drive integration — MCP created the standard and a distributed ecosystem built the connectors. The first movers recognized the pattern, saw the gap, and built the layer while incumbents planned. They learned things that later entrants had to rediscover: which failure modes are real, which use cases matter, which are theoretically appealing but practically useless. That learning compounds.

BotBeam

Here's the current reality. Right now, today, you can talk to ChatGPT or Claude about your entire digital life — email, docs, calendar, databases, two-way. That's the digital sphere, and it's mostly bridged. But you can't ask your agent anything about your car, your home, or whether your kids are back from school. The real-world sphere is untouched.

This isn't a technology limitation. Both Claude and ChatGPT support MCP integration. It's still choppy, it's not being pushed in-app, and awareness is low — but it's possible. The infrastructure exists. What doesn't exist is the other side: devices that are ready to participate.

Think about what's actually happening: one person, several agents, many resources — digital and physical. The agents need two-way interaction with all of them. The digital side is largely done. The physical side has barely started.

BotBeam is not a technology play. It's the open claw at this stage of the continuum. While incumbents move slowly, we find specific points where a thin layer of software — exposed via MCP — is enough to close the gap. Places where the distance between supply and demand is already minimal.

What does that look like in practice? Not controlling everything in your home. Maybe one persistent ambient display device — a small screen on your kitchen counter showing AI-curated information, updating throughout the day, controllable from any of your agents. Not locked to one ecosystem. Not requiring an enthusiast-level tolerance for configuration. Just a device that your agent can see and talk to, the same way it already sees your email and talks to your calendar.

That's deliberately small. The point isn't to boil the ocean. It's to be the enabling function at the specific points where the gap is narrowest — and to learn, fast, what it actually takes to make agents useful in the physical world.

The Broader Point

The reach continuum is a lens for understanding where opportunities emerge in AI infrastructure. Each unlock created value not because the underlying technology was new — it almost never was — but because someone built the connecting layer that made the technology accessible to agents. The web existed long before agents could browse it. Personal data APIs existed long before agents could use them. Physical devices exist now, and they're waiting for the same bridge.

The pattern will repeat. After physical, there will be another frontier — perhaps social (agents that interact with other agents on your behalf), perhaps institutional (agents that navigate bureaucratic systems), perhaps something nobody has named yet. Each time, the structure will be the same: the technology exists, the demand is forming, and the thin layer is missing.

The question is always the same. Where is the thin layer missing? And who builds it first?
